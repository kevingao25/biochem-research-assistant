# Biochem Research Assistant

This is a brief on what the product is *for*. The technical state lives in `PROGRESS.md`; this file is the *why*.

The user is my wife. She's a biochemistry PhD student researching phage-bacteria defense mechanisms, and like most working scientists, she struggles to keep up with her own field — not for lack of interest but for lack of energy. She subscribes to NCBI keyword alerts that drop ten or twenty paper titles into her inbox at a time. She scans them when she has the bandwidth, clicks the ones that look interesting, ignores the rest. It is a tedious, outdated workflow that pushes the cost of triage entirely onto the human. Reading papers is hard work in its own right. The friction of triage compounds with the friction of reading, and the backlog grows.

Both she and I have observed the same thing about ourselves. We don't click digest emails. We don't subscribe to TL;DR newsletters. The "send a smarter email" framing is dead on arrival.

So the product is not a smarter email. It is a push-based research curator with two surfaces: a short weekly briefing delivered to her inbox, and a web app where the curation lives in depth. The email is the briefing itself — actual synthesized prose, not a list of titles — with a link to the web for the cards, the per-paper experience, and the profile. Both surfaces render the same underlying selection: the system picks the small handful of papers worth her attention each week. The center of gravity is push, not pull; the system does triage on her behalf.

The product principle is the part that matters most, and it is the opposite of what most personalized recommendation systems do. Social media optimizes for stickiness, time-on-app, FOMO, streaks, infinite scroll. The incentive is to harvest attention, because that is how the platform makes money. This product is built for the inverse: protect her attention, don't draw it. Show fewer items, higher quality. Default to silence. No badges, no usage stats, no "you've missed 47 papers" framing. If the right answer is "go close the tab and read the paper directly," the system should say so. When in doubt during design, ask whether a feature draws attention or protects it, and cut the ones that draw.

This is opinionated software. It should not feel like a B2B SaaS dashboard or a generative-AI demo. It should feel like a thoughtful, quiet tool that someone made for one specific person.

The first time she opens the app, an agent greets her with a Q&A interview — five or ten minutes of open-ended questions about what she's working on, what she's read recently, what kinds of papers she usually skips. The agent synthesizes the conversation into a structured profile: a research statement in her own words (embedded as the primary interest vector), explicit lists of topics, authors, and labs, and any do-not-show signals. The profile is inspectable and editable on a settings page — transparency is mandatory. As she uses the product, the profile evolves: a like button on each paper card and per-paper page provides implicit signal, and her interactions during paper reading inform the profile over time. The profile is alive, and it's hers.

The briefing arrives weekly on a fixed day — predictable enough that she can ignore it without anxiety. Rarely, the system also sends an out-of-band email when a single paper crosses a high-confidence importance bar — a major preprint from a lab she explicitly follows, for instance. Most weeks that ad-hoc channel is silent. When a week is genuinely quiet, the system doesn't fall back to "you're caught up." It opens the lens slightly and surfaces something adjacent: a paper a little outside her core interests but close enough to be interesting. The empty state is itself a feature, not a fallback.

When she clicks a paper from the briefing or the curation page, the per-paper experience has two layers. First, an AI pre-read: the system has already analyzed the paper in her context and shows her the epistemic structure, the key insights, the methods, and a short specific paragraph on why this one matched her — the trust thread, never generic. Below that, the paper itself, embedded as a PDF reader with a chat scoped to that paper. She can read, ask the agent to explain methods, dig into related work, anything. Annotations and persistent highlights come later; v1 is read-only PDF plus chat that knows the paper.

Data sources are PubMed and bioRxiv — her current world plus the preprint frontier. arXiv q-bio gets demoted because it covers computational and theoretical biology, not the experimental work she actually does. Email-only for v1; WeChat or other channels are a later effort.

Success is when she trusts what the system surfaces enough to open it without prompting, and reports it saved her time. Engagement is explicitly not the metric — opening it more often is not better than opening it less often if the latter is the right amount. The agent's voice is a thoughtful colleague, not a chipper assistant: it speaks plainly, doesn't pad, doesn't apologize unnecessarily, and respects that she knows her field better than it does.

The existing build is the substrate, not throwaway. Airflow ingestion, Qdrant hybrid search, agentic RAG, Redis cache, Langfuse — all of it stays, all of it gets pointed at this new shape.
